Accuracy: how close are AI estimates?

Let's be straightforward: a selfie is not a DEXA scan. AI-based body composition estimates are visual approximations, not clinical measurements.

But they're surprisingly useful for what most gym-goers actually need ‚Äî tracking change over time.

You don't need to know your exact body fat percentage to three decimal places. You need to know: Am I making progress? Are my shoulders developing faster than my chest? Is my body fat trending down over six months?

GainFrame's AI estimates are most valuable when used comparatively ‚Äî your Week 1 Deep Dive versus your Week 12 Deep Dive. The relative change between consistent scans is far more actionable than any single measurement.

Think of it like this: a bathroom scale might not give you your exact weight to the ounce. But if you weigh yourself at the same time every week, the trend line tells you everything you need to know.

Deep Dive works the same way ‚Äî with a lot more data points than a scale.

How to get the best results

The AI works with any gym photo, but you'll get the most consistent and accurate estimates if you follow a few simple habits:

  1. Same lighting. Natural or consistent gym lighting beats a dark locker room. Shadows affect how muscle definition appears.
  2. Same pose. GainFrame organizes your photos by pose template ‚Äî Front, Back, Side, Front Flexed, etc. Stick to the same poses for your comparison photos.
  3. Same time of day. Morning photos after waking up tend to be the most consistent. Post-pump photos look great but add variance.
  4. Show enough of your body. The AI needs to see major muscle groups to score them. A neck-up selfie won't give you a full analysis.
  5. Use the Ghost Overlay Camera. GainFrame's built-in camera shows a transparent overlay of your last photo so you can match your pose exactly ‚Äî no tripod needed.
